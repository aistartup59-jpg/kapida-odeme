# ADR-012: Financial History Immutability

- **Status:** Accepted
- **Date:** 2026-07-14

## Context

Epic 5 (Cancel Payment Request Flow) required a decision on what happens to already-recorded monetary values when a PaymentRequest undergoes a lifecycle-only change, such as `PARTIALLY_PAID -> CANCELLED`. `PaymentStateMachineService` (ADR-011) already centralizes *who* may write `PaymentRequest.status`, but it says nothing about *what else* a status-changing operation may touch. Without an explicit rule, future lifecycle-affecting code — cancellation, expiry, and eventually refunds — risks silently rewriting `paidAmount` or historical `Transaction` rows, which would break auditability and reconciliation, and would undermine the derived `remainingAmount` guarantee already established in ADR-002.

## Decision

Financial records in Kapıda Ödeme are append-only.

- `Transaction` rows are immutable once persisted: no service may update or delete a `Transaction` after creation.
- `PaymentRequest.paidAmount` is immutable once recorded: it only ever advances by recording new `Transaction` rows through `TransactionEngineService`. No other operation, including a lifecycle transition, may modify it.
- A lifecycle transition applied through `PaymentStateMachineService.applyTransition()` (ADR-011) may change `PaymentRequest.status` and nothing else. It must never read, reset, or otherwise modify `paidAmount` or any `Transaction` row.
- Cancellation is a pure lifecycle transition: it moves `PaymentRequest.status` to `CANCELLED` and must not create, delete, or modify any `Transaction`, and must not alter `paidAmount`.
- Refunds must be represented as new, distinct financial events appended to the ledger (e.g. a new offsetting `Transaction`-level record), never by rewriting, deleting, or decrementing an existing `Transaction` or the historical `paidAmount` value already recorded. The exact refund event model is left to the future Refund epic; this ADR only fixes the append-only contract it must satisfy.
- `remainingAmount` remains fully derived (ADR-002) from the append-only financial history. It must never be corrected by mutating that history.
- This rule is provider neutral: it governs the internal financial ledger only, applies uniformly regardless of which `PaymentMethod` or `PaymentProvider` produced a given `Transaction`, and admits no provider-specific exception.

### Relationship to ADR-011

ADR-011 governs *who* may write `PaymentRequest.status` (only `PaymentStateMachineService`). ADR-012 governs *what* a status-changing operation may touch: a lifecycle transition may change status and nothing else. Together, the two ADRs mean no code path may combine a status change with a retroactive edit to money already recorded.

## Consequences

**Advantages:**

- The `Transaction` table remains a truthful, append-only history of what actually happened financially, at all times.
- The future Refund epic has a clear, unambiguous contract to build against: append, never rewrite.
- Removes ambiguity for cancellation, expiry, and any future lifecycle-affecting service about whether they may touch `paidAmount` or transaction history.

**Disadvantages:**

- Correcting a mistaken `Transaction` requires an explicit compensating entry rather than a direct edit, which is marginally more work in tooling and support flows.

## Alternatives Considered

- Zeroing or adjusting `paidAmount` on cancellation: rejected because it destroys the historical record of what was actually collected and breaks reconciliation.
- Allowing services to edit or delete `Transaction` rows directly (e.g. to implement refunds): rejected because it removes the append-only audit trail and reintroduces, at the `Transaction` level, the multiple-write-path problem ADR-011 was created to eliminate at the `status` level.
